Our mission

A free science library, built and governed by its community

Hivemind is a nonprofit dedicated to organizing the world's scientific knowledge — not for profit, not for algorithms, but for the people who want to learn.

What we are building

Most science knowledge is scattered — across paywalled journals, inconsistent Wikipedia stubs, and Reddit threads. Hivemind structures that knowledge into something students and researchers can actually navigate.

Each article contains a structured encyclopedia entry and a resource tree — a community-curated bubble map of textbooks, papers, and references organized by type.

Our principles

01
Free, forever
No ads, no paywalls, no subscription tiers. Knowledge should not cost money to access.
02
Community governed
Editors and contributors decide what belongs. No single institution controls the content.
03
Transparent sourcing
Every classification is debated openly. Disputes are visible, not hidden.
04
Anti-dependency by design
We build tools that make you a better, more independent learner.

Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that describes physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles.[1] It is the foundation of all quantum physics, including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.

Classical physics describes many aspects of nature at an ordinary (macroscopic) scale, but quantum mechanics is necessary to explain the behavior of matter and light at the atomic and subatomic scale.[2]

Overview

Quantum mechanics differs from classical physics in that energy, momentum, angular momentum, and other quantities of a bound system are restricted to discrete values (quantization); objects have characteristics of both particles and waves (wave–particle duality); and there are limits to how accurately physical quantities can be predicted prior to measurement (uncertainty principle).[3]

This article's resource tree organizes 38 textbooks and papers into primary sources, further reading, supplemental materials, and disputed sources.

History

The origins of quantum mechanics date to the 1838 discovery of cathode rays by Michael Faraday, followed by the 1859 black-body radiation problem and the 1900 quantum hypothesis by Max Planck.[4]
