Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that describes physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles.[1] It is the foundation of all quantum physics, including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.

Classical physics describes many aspects of nature at an ordinary (macroscopic) scale, but quantum mechanics is necessary to explain the behavior of matter and light at atomic and subatomic scales.[2]

Overview

Quantum mechanics differs from classical physics in that energy, momentum, angular momentum, and other quantities of a bound system are restricted to discrete values (quantization); objects have characteristics of both particles and waves (wave–particle duality); and there are limits to how accurately physical quantities can be predicted before measurement (uncertainty principle).[3]

Mathematical formulation

A mathematical function known as the wave function provides information about the probability amplitude of energy, momentum, and other physical properties of a particle.[4]

History

The origins of quantum mechanics date to the 1838 discovery of cathode rays by Michael Faraday, followed by the 1859 black-body radiation problem by Gustav Kirchhoff and the 1900 quantum hypothesis by Max Planck.[5]

Our mission

A free science library, built and governed by its community

Hivemind is a nonprofit dedicated to organizing the world's scientific knowledge — not for profit, not for algorithms, but for the people who want to learn.

What we are building

Most science knowledge is scattered — across paywalled journals, inconsistent Wikipedia stubs, and Reddit threads. Hivemind structures that knowledge into something students and researchers can navigate.

Each article contains a structured encyclopedia entry and a resource tree — a community-curated bubble map organized by type: primary, further reading, supplemental, and disputed sources.
