Our mission

A free science library, built and governed by its community

Hivemind is a nonprofit dedicated to organizing the world's scientific knowledge — not for profit, not for algorithms, but for the people who want to learn. We believe that knowing where to start is half the battle.

What we are building

Most science knowledge is scattered — across paywalled journals, inconsistent Wikipedia stubs, Reddit threads, and professors' syllabi that never make it online. Hivemind structures that knowledge into something students and researchers can actually navigate.

Each article on Hivemind contains two things: a structured encyclopedia entry about the topic, and a resource tree — a community-curated, navigable map of textbooks, papers, and references, organized by type: primary sources, further reading, supplemental materials, and disputed sources.

Our principles

01
Free, forever
No ads, no paywalls, no subscription tiers. Knowledge should not cost money to access.
02
Community governed
Editors and contributors decide what belongs. No single institution or funder controls the content.
03
Transparent sourcing
Every classification in the resource tree is debated openly. Disputes are visible, not hidden.
04
Anti-dependency by design
We build tools that make you a better, more independent learner — not more dependent on the platform.

Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that describes the physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles.[1] It is the foundation of all quantum physics, including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.

Classical physics describes many aspects of nature at an ordinary (macroscopic) scale, but quantum mechanics is necessary to explain the behavior of matter and light at the atomic and subatomic scale.[2]

Overview

Quantum mechanics differs from classical physics in that energy, momentum, angular momentum, and other quantities of a bound system are restricted to discrete values (quantization); objects have characteristics of both particles and waves (wave–particle duality); and there are limits to how accurately the value of a physical quantity can be predicted prior to its measurement (uncertainty principle).[3]

This article's resource tree contains 38 textbooks and papers organized into primary sources, further reading, supplemental materials, and disputed sources.

Mathematical formulation

The mathematical formulations of quantum mechanics are abstract. A mathematical function known as the wave function provides information about the probability amplitude of energy, momentum, and other physical properties of a particle.[4]

History

The origins of quantum mechanics date to the 1838 discovery of cathode rays by Michael Faraday, followed by the 1859 statement of the black-body radiation problem by Gustav Kirchhoff, the 1877 suggestion by Ludwig Boltzmann that energy states could be discrete, and the 1900 quantum hypothesis by Max Planck.[5]

Dirac's Principles is genuinely primary — it's where the formalism originates. Griffiths is an excellent pedagogical text, but pedagogical and primary are different. I'd keep it in further reading with a clear note explaining why.
